# ENGLISH SCRIPT

## Day 3: Troy’s Lost Layers — The Truth Behind the Myth and the Treasures Schliemann Stole

[Black screen. The distant sound of a war horn. Through flames, the shadow of ruined stone walls appears.]

**NARRATOR:**  
For thousands of years, people thought Troy was a legend.

A war that began because of beauty…  
Achaean armies crossing the seas…  
Unbreakable walls…  
And a wooden horse brought into the city under the cover of night.

But what if Homer’s story was not entirely fiction?

What if Troy really burned?  
What if people truly died behind those walls?  
And what if the greatest betrayal did not happen during the war… but thousands of years later, during the excavations?

[A misty view of Hisarlık Hill appears. The camera slowly transitions into an animation showing underground layers.]

Today, we travel to one of Anatolia’s most famous, and most damaged, mysteries: Troy.

This is not just an ancient city.  
This is a giant time capsule where mythology and history collide, and every strike of a pickaxe reveals another age.

Because Troy is not one city.

Troy is a memory mound made of cities built on top of cities.

[An animation appears: layers rising one above another — Troy I, II, III, VI, VII.]

Excavations at Hisarlık Hill showed that cities were founded here again and again across different periods. One city collapsed. Another was built on top of it. That one burned. Then another people arrived. New walls, new houses, new gates were constructed.

So the place we call “Troy” today is not a single answer.  
It is a stack of questions.

But the real question is this:

Which layer was Homer’s Troy?

[Black-and-white excavation photographs. Then dramatic battle illustrations inspired by the Iliad.]

In the 19th century, a wealthy and ambitious man named Heinrich Schliemann chased a dream he had believed in since childhood. To him, Troy was not merely a tale. It was a real place. And he was going to find it.

At the time, this idea was bold. Many scholars considered the Trojan War a poetic legend, and Homer’s account a mythological story.

Schliemann thought differently.

He had a pickaxe in his hand, money behind him, and Homer in his mind.

And the place he came to was Hisarlık Hill, in Çanakkale.

[Reenactment of workers excavating. Soil is removed rapidly.]

But here, one of the greatest problems in the history of archaeology began.

Because when Schliemann excavated, he was not only searching. He was rushing.

To reach Homer’s Troy, he removed the upper layers quickly. By today’s archaeological standards, he caused serious damage to many important levels. To him, the real treasure was deeper down.

But the tragedy was this:

In order to reach the Troy he was looking for, he may have destroyed some of its most important traces with his own hands.

[Animation of a deep excavation trench cutting through and destroying upper layers.]

Then one day, Schliemann announced that he had found gold, silver, jewelry, and precious objects. He called them “Priam’s Treasure.”

In other words, he claimed they belonged to King Priam of Troy.

The name was fascinating.  
It was perfect for newspapers.  
It was sensational for the world.

But there was one major problem.

Today, we know that the treasure most likely came from a layer much older than the period usually associated with Homer’s Trojan War. In other words, what Schliemann called “Priam’s Treasure” did not match the time in which Priam may have lived.

[Dramatic close-ups of gold jewelry. Then a timeline animation appears.]

Put simply, the treasure was real.  
But its story may have been wrong.

And the darker part was this:

The treasure was found on Ottoman land. But instead of handing it over through authorized channels, Schliemann secretly took it abroad.

Today, this event is seen as one of the most controversial cases of cultural property removal in the history of archaeology.

So Troy’s treasure was not only taken out of the ground.  
It was also torn away from its own land.

[Black screen. A soft chain sound. Then an empty museum display case appears.]

Here, the story suddenly changes.

Because now the question is no longer only, “Was Troy real?”

The question becomes:

Who owns the memory of a civilization?

The person who finds it?  
The museum that displays it?  
Or the country whose soil preserved it for thousands of years?

[Grass sways in the wind over Hisarlık Hill.]

Troy’s lost layers show us something else as well: mythology is not always a complete lie. Sometimes it is the shadow of real events, enlarged over generations until it becomes epic.

Maybe a war really happened.  
Maybe a city was truly besieged.  
Maybe a great fire really painted the sky red.

But the wooden horse?

Here, history falls silent, and interpretation begins.

[A silhouette of the wooden horse appears. Then quick animations of a siege tower, a ship, and an earthquake.]

According to some researchers, the Trojan Horse may not have been a literal wooden horse, but a symbolic description of a siege machine. Others see it as a military trick used to deceive the enemy. Some interpretations connect the “horse” to Poseidon, who was associated with both horses and earthquakes, suggesting that the fall of the city may also have been linked to a natural disaster.

What is certain is this:  
The story of Troy is not only a war story.

It is a story about how memory changes, how heroes are created, how defeats become epics, and how archaeology, while trying to uncover the truth, can sometimes destroy other truths.

[A reconstruction of Troy’s walls slowly appears. The sun is setting.]

When we look at Troy today, we see not only stones, but absences.

Lost layers…  
Misnamed treasures…  
Stolen artifacts…  
And a question that may never be fully answered:

Was Homer’s Troy truly here?

Maybe the answer is yes.  
Maybe the answer is no.  
Or maybe the most honest answer is this:

There was a city here.  
It was destroyed again and again.  
It was rebuilt again and again.  
And eventually, people told its story so many times that truth and legend could no longer be separated.

[Black screen. A golden mask, a burning city, and the silhouette of a wooden horse slowly overlap.]

The greatest mystery of Troy is not the wooden horse.

The greatest mystery is how much humanity changes the truth when it tells the past.

And Schliemann’s pickaxes remind us of this:

Sometimes finding a treasure does not illuminate history.  
Sometimes it wounds it forever.
